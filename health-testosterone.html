Testosterone

Description: Testosterone is the primary sex hormone responsible for the development of male physical characteristics, but it is essential for the health of all genders. Produced mainly in the testes for men and in the ovaries and adrenal glands for women, it regulates bone density, muscle mass, fat distribution, red blood cell production, and sex drive (libido).

Common Causes of Low T: Aging, obesity, injury to testes, chronic illness, certain medications.

COMMON SYMPTOMS OF IMBALANCE:
  • Low Testosterone: Persistent exhaustion, decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, muscle loss, increased body fat (especially in the belly), and "brain fog" or irritability are common symptoms.
  • High Testosterone: This can result in acne, aggressive behaviour, or a high red blood cell count in males; in women, it frequently shows up as thinning scalp hair, irregular periods, and abundant face or body hair (hirsutism).

Treatment: Testosterone replacement therapy, lifestyle changes, treating underlying conditions.

When To See A Doctor: If you have a chronic, months-long decrease in energy, drive, or sexual desire that cannot be attributed to transient stress or sleep deprivation, you should speak with a healthcare professional. Seeking medical counsel is especially crucial if you see physical "red flags" like the growth of breast tissue (gynaecomastia), weakening bones, or an inexplicable decrease of muscular power. Because testosterone levels fluctuate throughout the day and peak in the early hours, a morning blood test is required for a clinician to make a conclusive diagnosis. Low testosterone can occasionally be an early warning indicator of underlying metabolic problems like Type 2 diabetes or cardiovascular disease, therefore early management is crucial.

Tips: Exercise regularly, maintain healthy weight, get enough sleep, reduce stress.
